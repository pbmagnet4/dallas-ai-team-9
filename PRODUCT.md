# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Marketing managers and SEO leads at $1M–10M businesses with GA4 and Search Console connected. They know their business goals but don't live in data tools. Their job-to-be-done: "tell me which pages to fix first and why" — without wading through raw reports, spreadsheet exports, or five separate dashboards. They open NavFlow to answer one question: where are customers getting stuck, and what do I do about it?

## Product Purpose

NavFlow correlates Search Console impressions/clicks, GA4 sessions/conversions, and DataForSEO keyword rankings at the URL level — then renders the full customer journey as an interactive node canvas with AI-generated action briefs for each issue pattern. It turns multi-source diagnostic work that would take an analyst half a day into a five-minute "here's the problem, here's the fix" surface. Success: a user opens an audit, identifies the top 3 actions, and knows exactly what to hand to their team.

## Brand Personality

Bold · Direct · Competitive

NavFlow is a weapon, not a dashboard. It gives users a real edge over competitors who are flying blind on behavioral data. The interface should project: we know the answer. Not: here are some interesting numbers. Every screen should make the user feel like they're holding intelligence their competitors don't have.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS dashboards (Notion/Linear/Intercom):** No purple or indigo gradient washes. No "beautiful" empty-state illustrations. No confetti or delight-for-delight's-sake animations. No identical card grids with icon + heading + body copy.
- **SEO tools (Ahrefs/Semrush/Moz):** Not a data-dump table interface. Not cramped sidebar navigation with 40 options. Not a tool that shows every number it has because it can.
- **Google Analytics / Looker Studio:** Not a grey card grid. Not a government-report aesthetic. Not default charting library output. Not a tool that requires training to read.

## Design Principles

1. **The canvas is the product.** The journey map is the hero of every screen. Chrome exists to serve it, not compete with it.
2. **Insight over data.** Never show raw numbers when an action brief fits. A session count is noise; "290 sessions enter this page and 0% convert — fix the CTA" is signal.
3. **Confidence, not caveat.** The interface projects certainty: the answer is known. Hedging language ("might be," "could indicate") belongs in footnotes, not primary UI.
4. **Competitive framing.** Every metric is a score to beat, not a stat to observe. Users should leave each session feeling like they have an advantage.
5. **Scarcity of chrome.** Every pixel that isn't the journey map or an action brief is waste. Reduce until removing more would break clarity.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA minimum (4.5:1 body text, 3:1 large text)
- Reduced-motion alternative for every animation (crossfade or instant)
- Issue patterns must not rely on color alone — pair with labels and shape for colorblind users
- Keyboard navigable canvas interactions where possible
